What the map changed—and what it didn’t

A synthesis of the run-level cost data, the V1 findings handoff, and the exact diagnostic questions. The emphasis is on claims that survive scrutiny, question-level reversals hidden by averages, and follow-ups worth measuring.

Reading rule: IDX describes how the map was built; the retriever name describes who navigated it. Scores below are shown on the observed 0–4 scale. The handoff labels the rubric 0–5, so the authoritative rubric should be checked before publication.

The most defensible findings

These statements preserve the strongest parts of both analyses while avoiding claims the current design cannot support.

On this authored-hierarchy corpus, summary enrichment did not improve average answer quality for the competent cloud navigators.

Sonnet tied across all 13 completed D-versus-C pairs. GPT-4o’s IDX-C mean was 0.07 points below IDX-D. This is a null for average quality, not proof that summaries are generally useless.

The navigator mattered more on average—but the average hides large question-specific index effects.

For GPT-4o, 10 of 14 D-versus-C answers tied, while four moved sharply: DL5 +1, RD4 +2, EG4 −1, and CN6 −3. Summaries sometimes helped and sometimes redirected retrieval badly.

Sonnet’s advantage concentrated on scattered or conflicting evidence, not direct lookup.

Direct-location behavior was essentially tied. The useful contrast appeared when the task required broader exploration or preserving disagreement across sources.

Evidence-gap questions exposed complementary failure modes that a leaderboard would miss.

One navigator verified absence through a broad scan; another found an indirect trace. Neither completed both parts of EG4.

A richer map can exceed a smaller navigator’s practical context budget.

Qwen fetched no content on all 14 IDX-C runs. Because the measured structure was about 43,730 tokens and the model was labeled ctx32k, context compatibility must be investigated before calling this simply a model-quality failure.

The index tree—not the final answer—is the likely dominant cost term.

The logged floor was $1.29; the navigation-inclusive model was $34.83, about 26.9× higher. Exact cross-index dollars remain uncertain because each index’s structure size was not measured separately.

Claim-by-claim review of the alternate handoff

AGREE, WITH NARROWING

Navigator choice mattered more than summary enrichment on average

Among the competent cloud navigators, adding summaries produced no mean quality gain over the authored hierarchy. But “determined” is too absolute: index effects were strongly question-specific, and the experiment is small.

AGREE

Sonnet’s advantage had more than one mechanism

The traces support both deeper exploration on scattered-evidence questions and more faithful synthesis when conflicting evidence was already retrieved. The second mechanism remains lower-confidence because there were only two consistency questions.

AGREE AS A CASE STUDY

EG4 exposes a real two-part evidence-gap failure

The absence had to be verified and indirect traces had to be found. On IDX-D, GPT-4o performed the broad scan; Sonnet found one indirect trace; neither completed the full answer. This is illuminating, but it is one question rather than a measured general rate.

OTHER — IMPORTANT CONFOUND

Qwen entered a collapse regime

15 of 28 local runs fetched no content, including all 14 IDX-C runs. Calling this only a weak-tool-caller result is premature: the measured IDX-C structure was about 43,730 tokens while the model name indicates a 32K context window.

DISAGREE AS WRITTEN

Output length was not generally an inverse quality signal

The negative pattern is driven mainly by the collapsed Qwen runs. Within the cloud-model runs, longer output was not reliably worse. The defensible statement is that verbosity was not evidence of grounding.

OTHER — STRONG HYPOTHESIS, NOT YET A FINDING

Prompt caching is likely the largest cost lever

The repeated tree prefix makes caching technically promising, but the savings have not yet been measured. Treat cached-versus-uncached cost and latency as a follow-up experiment rather than a V1 result.

Score-scale correction: the handoff repeatedly says 0–5, while the observed response data contain scores from 0 through 4. Verify the rubric source and use one scale consistently.
Missing-run correction: Sonnet was identical on the 13 completed IDX-D/IDX-C pairs—not all 14. The IDX-D × Sonnet run for RD6 has no score.
Cost correction: fewer tool calls on IDX-C do not yet prove lower dollars because IDX-C’s structure may be larger than IDX-D’s.
Causal-language correction: “navigator determined quality” should become “navigator choice produced the larger average differences under these conditions.”

Two important findings missing from the handoff

There is an index–navigator interaction, not merely an index winner.

Across 13 completed Sonnet pairs, IDX-C preserved identical answer quality while using 1.31 fewer calls and 2.79 fewer seconds on average. With GPT-4o, IDX-C used the same mean number of calls, was slower on every question, and scored slightly lower.

The near-zero average for IDX-C hides volatility.

GPT-4o’s D-to-C changes ranged from −3 to +2. The important follow-up is not only “Did summaries help?” but “Which summary or branch changed the route for CN6, RD4, DL5, and EG4?”
